INTRODUCTION

I'm a high school student who is studying Linear Algebra with the Professor Gillbert Strang’s MIT OpenCourse. This is the code I used to practice linear algebra on.

This code includes most linear algebra operations as well as a basic interactive command line that allows users to perform computations.

To run this code, a python3 interpreter (3.6 and above) is required. And also, no 3rd library is used in this code.

  1. matrix(my_matrix):

to create a matrix, use matrix(your_matrix)

  • param: my_matrix: the matrix, use list[list] to input a matrix. Type: Union[int, float, complex] are supported as elements in matrix.

For example, To create:

X=[123234345]X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 3 & 4\\ 3 & 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix} X=⎣⎡​123​234​345​⎦⎤​

, type:

X = matrix([[1,2,3],[2,3,4],[3,4,5]])

 

 

Also, you could create a matrix with different types of element:

T=[cos(θ)−sin(θ)sin(θ)cos(θ)]θ=2πT = \begin{bmatrix} cos(\theta) & -sin(\theta)\\ sin(\theta) & cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \qquad \theta = 2\pi T=[cos(θ)sin(θ)​−sin(θ)cos(θ)​]θ=2π

, type:

theta = 2 * pi
T = matrix([[cos(theta), -sin(theta)], [sin(theta), cos(theta)]])

Or create:

A=[2+3i4+2i−1−2i3−4i]A = \begin{bmatrix} 2+3i & 4+2i\\ -1-2i & 3-4i \end{bmatrix} A=[2+3i−1−2i​4+2i3−4i​]

, type (use j or J as complex part):

A = matrix([[2+3j, 4+2j], [-1-2j, 3-4j]])

 

 

note, use double square braces to represent a matrix, even if your matrix has only one row.

For example:

X=[3456]X = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} X=[3​4​5​6​]

, type:

X = matrix([[3, 4, 5, 6]])

 

 

the operations between matrix (+, -, *) are supported.

to compute:

[122334][543224]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 2 & 3\\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 4 & 3\\ 2 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} ⎣⎡​123​234​⎦⎤​[52​42​34​]

, type:

matrix([[1,2],[2,3],[3,4]]) * matrix([[5,4,3],[2,2,4]])

  1. matrix.gauss_jordan_elimination(self)

perform a gauss jordan elimination and return the gauss jordan elimination matrix.

to solve the equation set:

{ x+2y+3z=4 2x+3y+4z=6 3x+3y+3z=4 x,y,z∈R\begin{cases} \ x+2y+3z=4 &\\ \ 2x+3y+4z=6 &\\ \ 3x+3y+3z=4 \end{cases} \ x, y, z \in \mathbb{R} ⎩⎪⎨⎪⎧​ x+2y+3z=4 2x+3y+4z=6 3x+3y+3z=4​​ x,y,z∈R

, transfer the equation set into matrix:

[123423463334]{\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\ 2 & 3 & 4 & 6\\ 3 & 3 & 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}} ⎣⎡​123​233​343​464​⎦⎤​

 

 

>>> X = matrix([[1,2,3,4],[2,3,4,6],[3,3,3,4]])

  1. matrix.combine(self, target)

Combine two matrices into one single matrix, the target matrix will appear at the right side of the given matrix.

For example, combine X and Y:

X=[113224343],Y=[433323213]X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 3\\ 2 & 2 & 4\\ 3 & 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} , Y= \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 & 3\\ 3 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} X=⎣⎡​123​124​343​⎦⎤​,Y=⎣⎡​432​321​333​⎦⎤​

The result is:

[113433224323343213]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 3 & 4 & 3 & 3\\ 2 & 2 & 4 & 3 & 2 & 3\\ 3 & 4 & 3 & 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} ⎣⎡​123​124​343​432​321​333​⎦⎤​

type:

>>> X = matrix([[1,1,3],[2,2,4],[3,4,3]]); Y = matrix([[4,3,3],[3,2,3],[2,1,3]])
>>> print(X.combine(Y))
matrix:
[1, 1, 3, 4, 3, 3]
[2, 2, 4, 3, 2, 3]
[3, 4, 3, 2, 1, 3]

Note

  1. All private and static method (starts with __ ) will overwrite the input matrix which indicates the parameter 'my_matrix'. If you want to remain the matrix unchanged, please use copy.deepcopy() or call the methods by <class '__main__.matrix'> object.

  2. But static methods are NOT SUITABLE for interactive programming and operation outside <class '__main__.matrix'>.
